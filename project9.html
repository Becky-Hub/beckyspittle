Design and Development of a Wirelessly Controlled Microphone Stand

This project was conducted for my PhD and investigates how proxemic dimensions—distance, orientation, movement, identity, and location—could be harnessed to enhance interactions in XR environments. By understanding the advantages, limitations, and affordances of commonplace interaction techniques, the research explores how we can provide more intuitive, adaptive, and contextually aware XR systems that improve user experience, interaction flow, and efficiency.

Project Details

The research involved three studies:

  • Study 1: Comparing Interaction Techniques within the Intimate Proxemic Zone (within arms reach)
    • Interaction Techniques: Hand Press, Hand Hover, Eye Gaze, and Head Gaze.
    • Tasks: Participants performed a series of "observe and interact" selection tasks.
    • Data Collected: Selection time, error rate, task load, user experience, and preference.
  • Study 2: Comparing Interaction Techniques across Personal, Social, and Public Zones (beyond arms reach)
    • Interaction Techniques: Hand Airtap, Hand Hover, Eye Gaze, and Head Gaze.
    • Tasks: Same selection tasks as Study 1, presented randomly across three proxemic zones.
    • Data Collected: Same metrics as Study 1, with focus on how distance impacts techniques.
  • Study 3: Exploring User-Defined Distance and Locomotion with different Interaction Techniques
    • Interaction Techniques:Hand Airtap, Hand Hover, Eye Gaze, and Head Gaze.
    • Tasks: Selection of virtual objects in a room-scale environment.
    • Data Collected: User-defined position, locomotion speed, selection time, task load, user experience, and preference, with focus on how object size impacts techniques.

Key Findings and Impact

The findings revealed significant insights:

  • Distance Impacts Techniques: The appropriateness of interaction techniques varied significantly with distance.
  • Users Adapt their Behaviours Based on Technique: Users naturally adjusted interaction approaches based on the affordances of techniques, preferring not to expend extra energy to approach objects physically.
  • Potential for Adaptive Interactions: There is potential to enhance user experience and efficiency via adaptive systems, which switch techniques based on dimensions of proxemic interaction.
  • User Preferences Vary: Users have distinct preferences for interaction techniques regardless of distance and context, emphasizing the value of personalised XR systems.

The research demonstrates the potential of leveraging proxemic dimensions to inform the design of more flexible and practical XR interactions, offering routes towards more impactful user experiences.
